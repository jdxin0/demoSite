The English Character

To other Europeans, the best known quality of (最著名的特点是)the British,and in particular of the English, is "reserved".A reserved(保守) person is one who does not talk very much to strangers,does not show much emotion, and seldom(很少，很难得) gets excited(激动的，兴奋的).

It is difficult to get to know a reserved person:he never tells you anything about himself,and you may work with him for years without ever knowing where he lives,how many children he has, and what his interests are.

English people tend to be（倾向于） like that. Closely related to(紧密相关的) English reserve is English modesty. Within their hearts, the English are perhaps no less conceited(自负) than anybody else,but in their relations with others they value(重视) at least a show of modesty(谦逊)但在他人交往中，他们注重谦逊，起码要表现出谦逊的姿态..

Self－praise自夸、自诩(赞美、赞扬) is felt to be impolite. If a person is, let us say,very good at tennis and someone asks him if he is a good player,he will seldom(很少) reply "Yes,"because people will think him conceited( 自负的；狂想的；逞能的). He will probably give an answer like,"I'm not bad," or "I think I'm very good," or "Well, I'm very keen on(热爱) tennis." Even if he had managed to(设法达成、达成（完成某事）) reach the finals（进入总决赛） in last year's local championships(本地锦标赛),he would say it in such a way as to suggest that(以表明) it was only due to a piece of good luck.

Since reserve and modesty are part of his own nature（天性）,the typical English tends to expect（往往期望） them in others. He secretly looks down on more excitable nations,and likes to think of himself as more reliable(可靠的) than they are. He doesn't trust big promises(言过其实的许诺) and open shows of feelings(过于外漏的情感),especially if they are expressed in flowery language(华丽的辞藻). He doesn't trust self－praise of any kind. This applies（这一点） not only to what other people may tell him about themselves orally(口头上地；口述地),but to the letters they may write to him. To those who are fond of(爱好；喜爱) flowery expressions,the Englishman may appear（显得） uncomfortably cold.
